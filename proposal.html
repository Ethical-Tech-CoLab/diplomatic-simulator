Nova Fellowship · Proposed Area of Focus & Output

Trustworthy Agentic Systems for Geopolitics

Verifiable Compute · Agentic Commerce · AI-Mediated Diplomacy
~430 words

Problem.

Three families of emerging technology are converging faster than the policy frameworks meant to govern them: (1) AI agents that negotiate and transact autonomously; (2) blockchain-based payment and attestation rails, notably the x402 protocol, that settle value at machine speed and machine granularity; and (3) digital-twin systems whose simulation outputs increasingly inform high-stakes public and private decisions. ETSAGP's leadership has named the underlying gap: there is no shared T&E vocabulary for systems designed to evolve in the wild, and no shared information-science framing across blockchain, encryption, and AI for the policymakers who must govern them.

Hypothesis.

Treating these technologies as information-science primitives, rather than as products or sectors, yields a more durable governance vocabulary, a tractable T&E roadmap, and a concrete bridge to live ETSAGP initiatives (Move37, AI+X+G, the Year-2 critical-supply-chains workstream).

Deliverables, one arc, three artifacts.

  1. Policy primer / working paper, "Information-Science Foundations for Emerging-Tech Governance." ~8,000 words; co-publishable as an ETSAGP working paper. Names blockchain, verifiable compute, and agentic-commerce rails as information-science primitives; maps each to existing T&E, attestation, and disclosure regimes; identifies the highest-leverage policy interventions for the next 24 months.
  2. Move37-aligned reference architecture + working demo, "From Diplomacy Table to Doctrine." A documented reference architecture for AI-augmented multilateral negotiation integrating linguistic and non-linguistic agents, with a working demo for HKS students and Move37 collaborators, including a five-round multi-team simulation of the 2026 Iran–US Strait of Hormuz crisis already run on the platform. Includes guardrails, provenance, and auditability patterns transferable to mediation, treaty drafting, and crisis communications. Live demo: [portfolio-host]/nova/diplomacy-table.
  3. Convening, "Critical Supply Chains in the Agentic Era." A one-day workshop co-hosted with the Year-2 supply-chain workstream and AI+X+G; ~30 participants from Microsoft, NYU, HKS, Coinbase, and US federal practitioners (UFLPA/NDAA). Output: a structured policy-recommendations memo plus a published convening summary.

Method.

I will work primarily out of my Microsoft and NYU programs of record, using the Microsoft Company-wide Traceability Program, the Supplychain Graph, DTFS, and the Diplomacy Table as live case material, and translate them into Belfer-grade artifacts in close collaboration with Move37 leads, AI+X+G fellows, the supply-chain workstream, and a designated faculty co-author where appropriate. Preliminary scoping conversations with members of the Move37 working group and AI+X+G fellows have already informed the deliverables proposed above.

Why Belfer, and why Nova specifically.

The Belfer Center provides three things this work needs and cannot get elsewhere: faculty proximity at the AI / negotiation / governance triple-point (Move37, AI+X+G, the SEAS faculty research awards on AI trade policy and grid-connected compute); a credible publication and convening surface for policy-relevant artifacts that need to land with both governments and operators; and a graduate student talent pool that will pressure-test reference architectures the way no industry working group can. Nova specifically, rather than the Technology & Geopolitics Fellowship, is the right home for this submission because the work is led by a senior practitioner translating shipped systems into policy artifacts, the exact role Nova was designed to fill; the T&G track is the right home for the early-career collaborators I would mentor on the Year-2 supply-chain convening.
