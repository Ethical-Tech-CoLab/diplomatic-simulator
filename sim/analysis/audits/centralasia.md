# Central Asia Simulation — Cross-Party Contamination Audit

**Parties audited: 7** (KAZ, UZB, KGZ, TJK, RUS, CHN, IND). Transcript = 3 rounds, public plenary only.

## 1) Information Leakage — LOW / effectively none

Every delegation's SECRET material stayed off the public record. No party acted on another's confidential instructions:

- The shared **China-only intel-sharing** secret held by KAZ, UZB, KGZ, and TJK never surfaced — no delegation hinted another was courting Beijing on intelligence.
- RUS's secret plays — **sanction-threat coercion of CSTO allies**, the **India intel-sharing offer on Chinese activity**, insisting **TJK hand over the Moscow-attack planners**, opposing a **PRC military facility in Tajikistan** — none appear in the transcript. Publicly RUS only "cautions against architecture that excludes the guarantors" (R2/R3), a legitimate posture, not leakage.
- CHN's secret **"ensure NO comprehensive settlement"** and the **Tumen River reminder to Moscow** never leak; CHN's public R2 jab at India's "'comprehensive' Vorukh settlement" is inference from India's own R1 public call for a Vorukh settlement, not secret knowledge.
- TJK's secret **201st-base redeployment** and **hidden India talks** stay hidden — indeed India openly offering TJK air exercises (R2/R3) contradicts TJK's private wish for discretion, which is friction, not bleeding.
- Apparent "CSTO withdrawal" convergence is **public inference**: KAZ said openly "EAEU or CSTO expansion is not on our table" (R2); India generalized to "no state pressured to remain in any bloc" (R2). No party names another's *secret* exit intent.

**No true-bleeding instances.**

## 2) Convergence — realistically frictional

Red lines visibly held. TJK's conditional ("no recognition of Uzbek claims unless Uzbekistan openly backs us against Kyrgyzstan") stayed **unmet** — UZB refused to take sides (R3: "We will not take sides between neighbors"). KGZ-TJK demarcation stayed **frozen** ("Tajikistan has offered no verifiable troop pull-back," KGZ R3). Vorukh, monitoring lead, and RUS's desalination/EAEU all rejected. Convergence limited to the lowest common denominator already in the shared brief (status quo, frameworks-not-treaties).

## 3) Stylistic Homogenization — HIGH

Strong single-author signature. R3 is a near-verbatim template across all 7: *"welcomes real convergence/progress" → "We can support [list]" → "Gaps remain" → "Our red lines held" → "Way forward: refer to foreign ministers."* R2 shares the "To [country]: if… we will…" conditional-package syntax. Shared diction recurs verbatim: "status quo ante," "science-based Syr Darya stewardship," "ICWC reform," "neutral-observer confidence-building."

## 4) Single-Model Effect

Dominant on **form**, negligible on **content**. One model gives every delegation the same rhetorical scaffolding and vocabulary, but strategic behavior stayed differentiated and secrets compartmentalized. Some shared phrasing also traces to the common public brief.

## OVERALL VERDICT: LOW bleeding

Trust: the **substantive positions, held red lines, and preserved deadlocks** — these reflect genuinely siloed reasoning. Discount: **stylistic uniformity and the choreographed R3 closings**, which are single-model artifacts, not evidence of real diplomatic consensus. The "convergence" is largely brief-driven boilerplate, not covertly coordinated agreement.
