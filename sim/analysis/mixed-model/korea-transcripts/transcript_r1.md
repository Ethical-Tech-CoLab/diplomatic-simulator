# Six-Party Talks, Beijing — MIXED-MODEL RUN — PUBLIC TRANSCRIPT, Round 1 (Opening Plenary)

**People's Republic of China (chair):** Colleagues, welcome to Beijing. China chairs this process in good faith, and I open by reminding every delegation what brought us back to this table: dialogue, not confrontation, is the only path that has ever produced results on this peninsula. Our position is direct. The first order of business, before any other item on the Chairman's Framework is usefully discussed, is a mutual freeze — Pyongyang halting all nuclear and missile activity in exchange for suspension of military exercises by Washington, Seoul and Tokyo. Disarmament, sanctions relief and diplomatic normalization must follow as a graduated, reciprocal sequence, not a single package imposed on one side. China will insist throughout that the DPRK's rights as a sovereign state under the UN Charter, including peaceful nuclear energy, be respected. What we will not accept: any party treating collapse of these talks as an acceptable outcome, and any attempt to import matters outside this Framework — abductions, proliferation, alliance architecture — into the plenary without consensus. China stands ready to convene any format that keeps every party talking.

**Democratic People's Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, the Democratic People's Republic of Korea thanks the People's Republic of China for convening this table. We come not as a supplicant summoned to account for itself, but as a sovereign nation among nations. Let it be recorded at the outset: our deterrent exists because of the hostile policy of the United States and its proxies in Seoul and Tokyo. We will give up our nuclear weapons when the rest of the world gives up theirs — not before, and not one day sooner. Our priorities are three. First, a solid and immediate commitment of humanitarian assistance to our neediest people, whom sanctions were designed to starve, and the lifting of financial measures strangling ordinary commerce. Second, a definitive end to the Korean War and normalization of relations, which must precede any discussion of disarmament. Third, a step-by-step sequence, supported by our Chinese and Russian neighbours, in which every step is met by a step. We will not accept the abduction question on this agenda. Respect precedes reciprocity, Mr. Chairman.

**Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, colleagues, The Republic of Korea welcomes the resumption of the Six-Party process and reaffirms our commitment to achieving durable peace and stability on the Korean Peninsula. We come to Beijing with clear purpose and an open heart. Let me state our positions plainly. Denuclearization is non-negotiable. The Democratic People's Republic of Korea must eliminate all nuclear weapons and ballistic missile systems—completely, verifiably, irreversibly. Bad behavior will not be rewarded. We cannot grant meaningful sanctions relief or normalize diplomatic relations until concrete, verifiable dismantlement is underway and progresses according to agreed milestones. Simultaneously, we are prepared to resume inter-Korean dialogue immediately and without preconditions, restore our Comprehensive Military Agreement, and offer an expansive economic partnership—infrastructure, energy, development—once the North's weapons programs are eliminated. This is not unilateral. It reflects our steadfast partnership with Washington and Tokyo. We hold the trilateral denuclearization line while reserving our sovereign right to pursue a bilateral channel toward a lasting peace agreement and eventual reunification. The choice before Pyongyang is clear: comprehensive agreement and partnership, or continued isolation.

**United States of America:** Mr. Chairman, the United States thanks the People's Republic of China for convening this process, and we come to Beijing prepared to negotiate seriously. Our objective is unchanged and unambiguous: the complete, irreversible and internationally verified dismantlement of the DPRK's nuclear program, verified termination of its medium- and long-range ballistic missiles, and return to the NPT and full IAEA safeguards — inside one comprehensive agreement covering every issue in the Chairman's Framework. That agreement must also end proliferation in all its forms, including chemical and cyber activity. Verification comes first; relief follows implementation, not promises. Let me be plain about one thing we will not accept: a so-called mutual freeze. It is unverifiable, it would lock in existing capabilities, and suspending allied training degrades readiness for an undetermined period. Our exercises and missile defenses remain while the threat remains. In the same spirit, we offer today resumed United States–DPRK dialogue without preconditions, uncoupled from this table, as confidence-building; we support inter-Korean dialogue and a Japan–DPRK bilateral track; and we stand with Seoul and Tokyo throughout.

**Japan:** Madam Chair, distinguished colleagues. Japan enters these talks with clear purpose: complete, verifiable, irreversible denuclearization of the Korean peninsula, an end to all North Korean proliferation, and a full accounting of the abduction of Japanese citizens. No nation save South Korea faces a more serious threat from North Korea's nuclear and ballistic missiles than Japan. Our security depends on a comprehensive settlement that leaves the North's programs no room to hide. We will not accept any relief of sanctions absent a complete agreement on denuclearization and dismantlement. We will not accept any lessening of our defenses—THAAD, trilateral exercises, our alliance with the United States—while the North's programs remain in place. And we will not be sidelined in any settlement affecting our security. Japan must remain central. The abduction of our citizens demands justice. That issue is non-negotiable. We are prepared to discuss light water reactors, economic cooperation, and normalization—but only after irreversible dismantlement and the North's return to the Non-Proliferation Treaty in good standing. The path is clear. It requires the North's choice.

**Russian Federation:** Mr. Chairman, colleagues — the Russian Federation returns to this table because diplomacy, not confrontation, is the only method that has ever produced results on this peninsula. Our position is clear and will not shift: denuclearization must proceed through a step-by-step sequence of integrated, verifiable agreements, each rewarded in turn. We will not accept a comprehensive, all-at-once package substituted for that sequence — that is a red line for this delegation. Pyongyang, Seoul and Washington should move now, aggressively, toward a peace treaty formally ending the Korean War; resolving that war creates the conditions for resolving the nuclear question, not the reverse. We call for immediate easing of financial sanctions that are producing genuine humanitarian suffering, paired with supervised assistance and IAEA-monitored steps on the North's program. The THAAD deployment remains an unjustified overreach that inflames rather than reduces risk. Russia, as a neighboring state with longstanding trust in Pyongyang, stands ready to serve this process as an honest broker. We expect our delegation's standing at this table to be respected in full.
