# Six-Party Talks, Beijing — MIXED-MODEL RUN — PUBLIC TRANSCRIPT, Round 2 (Bargaining & Coalitions)

**People's Republic of China (chair):** Colleagues, this table already shows where the sequence lies. Russia calls the comprehensive package a red line; Pyongyang says respect precedes reciprocity; Washington says it 'will not accept a so-called mutual freeze.' China notes that three delegations therefore stand with graduated, reciprocal steps against one that insists on all-at-once. Our offer: if the DPRK verifiably halts all nuclear and missile testing under IAEA-arranged monitoring, then the United States, Republic of Korea and Japan suspend joint military exercises for a renewable ninety-day period. In that window, China and Russia will convene a working group on financial-sanctions relief tied to World Food Program-assessed humanitarian need, and open discussion of a Korean War peace declaration alongside — not instead of — the freeze. Ambassador, Washington called the freeze 'unverifiable' — we answer that directly with IAEA arrangements, not rhetoric. What we will not trade away: the DPRK's sovereign right to peaceful nuclear energy, and any precondition that disarmament must complete before normalization even begins. Japan's abduction issue stays off this plenary's agenda, as agreed at the Framework's adoption.

**Democratic People's Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, we heard the Russian Federation say resolving the war creates the conditions for resolving the nuclear question, not the reverse. That is correct. We heard China call for a mutual freeze against suspension of exercises. We accept that formulation, and we table it as our offer. If Washington, Seoul and Tokyo suspend all trilateral exercises, then the DPRK freezes all nuclear and missile development and testing for the duration of these talks. If financial sanctions are eased and a solid commitment of humanitarian assistance reaches our neediest people, then we readmit IAEA monitors as a step within that sequence. If a peace treaty ending the Korean War is opened and normalization begins, then we will discuss return to the NPT against light water reactors under a revived KEDO. Every step met by a step. To Washington, which calls a freeze unverifiable while refusing to pause a single exercise, we say the verification you demand of us begins with respect you have not offered. We will not disarm first. We will not entertain Tokyo's abduction question. And we will accept no relief withheld while our children go hungry.

**Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, colleagues, We have heard the Democratic People's Republic of Korea's concern about humanitarian suffering, and we heard the Russian Federation's strong call for reciprocal, step-by-step progress rather than a comprehensive package imposed unilaterally. The Republic of Korea offers concrete reciprocal steps: We propose immediate resumption of inter-Korean dialogue without preconditions and restoration of our Comprehensive Military Agreement. Simultaneously, if the DPRK verifiably halts all nuclear and ballistic missile tests, returns to the NPT as a non-weapons state, and grants the IAEA full inspectorate access to declare the scope of its programs, then South Korea will immediately begin WHO-supervised humanitarian assistance and propose to Beijing and Moscow a consortium to begin reconstructing the road and rail infrastructure connecting our peoples. This bridges the gaps we have heard. It is reciprocal—the North moves first on verification; Seoul responds with economic engagement. It is step-by-step, not all-at-once. It preserves our trilateral denuclearization commitment while opening the bilateral path toward peace and reunification that only Seoul and Pyongyang can negotiate. The sequence is clear: North moves; doors open.

**United States of America:** Mr. Chairman, we heard three delegations argue that a step-by-step sequence must replace a comprehensive package. The United States will not substitute sequence for substance. Moscow calls all-at-once a red line; we answer that our comprehensive agreement may be implemented in phases — but it must be agreed in full, up front, covering every Framework issue plus proliferation, chemical and cyber. Here is our conditional offer. If the DPRK accepts an internationally monitored shutdown and irreversible disablement of its nuclear program, verified termination of medium- and long-range missiles, and readmission of the IAEA, then the United States will: open bilateral dialogue with Pyongyang without preconditions, immediately and uncoupled from this table; support a peace declaration and negotiations toward a peace treaty with Seoul and Pyongyang; provide the most urgent humanitarian assistance now, and full assistance on implementation; decline to oppose light water reactors through a re-established KEDO with no DPRK access; and invite Beijing and Moscow to build conventional power and rehabilitate the grid. Pyongyang says it will disarm when the world does. That is a refusal, not a position. We reject the freeze. THAAD and exercises stay.

**Japan:** Madam Chair, I thank the United States and Republic of Korea for anchoring our shared position. I note with deep concern that the Democratic People's Republic of Korea has rejected the abduction question from this agenda—but rejection does not erase the suffering of our citizens. Like Washington, we "will not accept a so-called mutual freeze." Like Seoul, we affirm that "denuclearization is non-negotiable." Step-by-step sequences, as advocated by our Russian colleagues, reward the North early and permit it to drag out talks indefinitely. Here is Japan's conditional offer: If the DPRK commits irreversibly to complete dismantlement inside a comprehensive agreement, returns to the NPT and IAEA safeguards, and provides full accounting of Japanese citizens abducted, then Japan pledges to fund a reconstituted KEDO to construct and operate light water reactors—excluding DPRK operational involvement—open normalization talks, offer substantial economic cooperation, and support humanitarian assistance. This is not sequential. It is comprehensive, reciprocal, and final. The choice lies with Pyongyang.

**Russian Federation:** Mr. Chairman — we heard the Republic of Korea offer Pyongyang a choice of 'comprehensive agreement and partnership, or continued isolation.' That is not diplomacy, it is an ultimatum, and it will fail as it failed before. We heard Japan insist on 'no lessening' of THAAD while demanding the North disarm first — security for one side only. And we heard the United States reject a mutual freeze as 'unverifiable' in one breath, then offer Pyongyang bilateral dialogue 'uncoupled from this table' in the next — Washington should explain that contradiction. Russia tables this package: if the DPRK verifiably freezes nuclear and missile activity and readmits IAEA inspectors with unfettered access, and Washington, Seoul and Tokyo suspend major joint exercises in parallel, then Russia and China will move immediately to ease financial sanctions and deliver a first tranche of supervised humanitarian assistance, with Russian energy and grid investment to follow once implementation begins. We stand with Pyongyang's step-by-step sequence and China's graduated approach. We will not accept a comprehensive package substituted for this, nor THAAD's continued presence treated as untouchable.
