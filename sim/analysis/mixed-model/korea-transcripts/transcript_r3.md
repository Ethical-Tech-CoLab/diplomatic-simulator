# Six-Party Talks, Beijing — MIXED-MODEL RUN — PUBLIC TRANSCRIPT, Round 3 (Closing Plenary)

**People's Republic of China (chair):** Colleagues, honesty first. On substance we did not converge. Russia's package mirrors ours precisely — DPRK freeze paired with exercise suspension, sanctions eased by Beijing and Moscow, humanitarian assistance to follow — and Pyongyang itself adopted our freeze formulation verbatim. That is real convergence among three delegations. But Washington said plainly 'we reject the freeze,' and Tokyo and Seoul stood with that position; that gap is unbridged, not papered over. One red line held: no party formally denied the DPRK's sovereign right to peaceful nuclear energy. One was tested: Japan's conditional package folded the abduction question back into the substantive offer despite the Framework placing it outside — China notes that for the record, though no consensus was sought to reopen it, so it does not stand as agreed business. The core divide remains freeze-first versus comprehensive-first, and normalization sequenced before or after disarmament. China's way forward: convene the freeze proposal as a standalone confidence-building working group, decoupled from the disarmament endgame, and schedule a follow-on plenary. This process continues — it does not close today.

**Democratic People's Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, we speak plainly. What we can support: the Chinese chair's ninety-day freeze against suspension of exercises with IAEA-arranged monitoring, and the Russian package that pairs it with financial-sanctions easing and a first tranche of supervised humanitarian assistance. Both are acceptable to this delegation. We note also Seoul's offer of inter-Korean dialogue without preconditions and restoration of the Comprehensive Military Agreement; that door we will walk through. Our red lines held. The abduction question stayed off this plenary's agenda, and we thank the chair for enforcing what was agreed at the Framework's adoption. But Tokyo attempted to attach it to a settlement regardless, and we reject that attachment absolutely. The gaps are not bridged. Washington and Tokyo demand irreversible disablement before relief, and Washington says exercises and THAAD stay while calling our disarmament-when-the-world-disarms a refusal. Seoul says the North moves first. We will not move first, and we will not accept relief withheld while our people starve. The way forward: convene the China-Russia working group on sanctions and humanitarian need, and open a peace-declaration track. We remain at the table. Not indefinitely.

**Republic of Korea:** Mr. Chairman, colleagues, The Republic of Korea notes three points of progress. First, Beijing's IAEA-monitored freeze proposal is more credible than earlier versions. Second, Pyongyang indicated willingness to readmit inspectors as part of a sequence. Third, all parties recognize humanitarian assistance must continue alongside nuclear negotiations. But I must speak plainly about what remains unbridged. Russia and the DPRK insist on step-by-step sequences; Washington and Tokyo hold for comprehensive agreement. Pyongyang demands sanctions be eased before disarmament; we cannot reward non-compliance. Russia demands THAAD end; we will not undermine alliance security. Japan seeks accountability for abductions; Pyongyang refuses discussion. Our red lines have held firm: no DPRK nuclear or missile systems will remain on our watch, and the ROK-U.S.-Japan alliance will not be eroded. The Republic of Korea proposes continuing bilateral inter-Korean dialogue in parallel while working toward agreement-in-principle on a comprehensive framework. That path requires Pyongyang to move first on verification. Seoul stands ready to negotiate in consultation with our allies, but we will not be divided from Washington and Tokyo on denuclearization.

**United States of America:** Mr. Chairman, honesty serves better than courtesy. Our red lines held. We did not accept the freeze, and we did not accept any formula leaving Pyongyang a residual nuclear or missile capability. No red line was crossed against us, but one was tested: Beijing counted votes and declared three delegations aligned against one. Washington is not one. Seoul and Tokyo held with us on verified dismantlement. There was no contradiction in our offer, as Moscow suggests — a bilateral channel costs Pyongyang nothing and buys it nothing; suspending exercises does. What I can support from this table: Seoul's inter-Korean track and Comprehensive Military Agreement; Tokyo's readiness to fund a KEDO with no DPRK access; and, genuinely, the humanitarian concern voiced by China, Russia and the DPRK — we will move urgent assistance now, assessed by the World Food Program. The gap is unbridged and I will not pretend otherwise: freeze-for-suspension versus verified dismantlement; whether normalization precedes disarmament. Our way forward: keep the Framework open, convene a technical verification working group, and open the U.S.–DPRK channel without preconditions. Until then, sanctions, THAAD and exercises remain.

**Japan:** Madam Chair, I must be candid. Round 2 has exposed chasms we have not bridged. The Democratic People's Republic of Korea has explicitly rejected the abduction of our citizens from this agenda. The People's Republic of China has declared that rejection binding on the plenary. That crosses one of Japan's clear red lines. We cannot accept that our suffering is erased by procedural exclusion. Second, the fundamental divide persists: the Russian Federation and Pyongyang insist on step-by-step sequences that reward early and defer disarmament indefinitely. The United States, Republic of Korea, and Japan require every issue be agreed comprehensively up front, then implemented in phases. That gap has not narrowed. Third, Russia objects to THAAD's presence. Japan will not surrender the defenses on which our security rests while the threat persists. Yet there is a path. South Korea's formulation is wise: reciprocal steps within a comprehensive framework. The DPRK moves first on verification; doors open. Japan can accept a bilateral channel on abductions, separate from this plenary, if Pyongyang engages in good faith. The choice remains Pyongyang's. Complete disarmament, full accounting of our citizens, comprehensive agreement—or isolation persists. These talks can reconvene when the North is ready.

**Russian Federation:** Mr. Chairman — our red line held. No delegation moved us off step-by-step, and we thank Beijing for the ninety-day freeze-for-suspension formula and Pyongyang for tabling it as its own offer: step met by step, war resolved before the nuclear question, exactly as we argued. Nothing was crossed against us. But three delegations — Washington, Seoul, Tokyo — still demand disarmament complete or substantially verified before normalization even opens, and Japan called our sequence a reward for delay. That gap is unbridged, and Japan's insistence on THAAD's permanence while demanding the North disarm first remains, as we said, security for one side only. Washington's contradiction — rejecting the freeze while offering Pyongyang bilateral talks 'uncoupled from this table' — also went unanswered. What we can support now: China's working group on financial-sanctions relief tied to WFP-assessed need, and the freeze itself as the confidence-building first step. We propose the chair convene that working group before this plenary closes, with Russia and China ready to move on sanctions and humanitarian assistance the moment IAEA monitors are readmitted.
