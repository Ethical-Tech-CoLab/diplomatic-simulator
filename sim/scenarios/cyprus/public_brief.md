# The Cyprus Crisis: Reunification Talks

## Current situation

Cyprus has been divided since Türkiye's 1974 invasion, which followed a coup against President Makarios and left Turkish forces controlling roughly 37% of the island behind the UN-monitored Green Line, with some 200,000 people displaced. In 1983 the Turkish Cypriot leadership declared the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus, an entity UN Security Council Resolution 541 called "legally invalid" and which only Türkiye recognizes. Decades of UN-mediated efforts to build a bi-zonal, bi-communal federation have failed, most notably when Greek Cypriots rejected the 2004 Annan Plan in referendum while Turkish Cypriots approved it; the Republic of Cyprus then joined the EU as a divided island. Talks collapsed again at Crans-Montana in 2017. The internationally recognized Republic of Cyprus (Greek Cypriot led) seeks reunification; the TRNC, backed by Türkiye, now presses for "sovereign equality" and a two-state solution. Recurring disputes over natural-gas exploration in Cyprus's Exclusive Economic Zone, the future of the UK Sovereign Base Areas, and the mandate of the UNFICYP peacekeeping force keep the crisis volatile. Delegations convene under UN auspices to seek a way forward.

## Negotiation issues on the table

1. SOVEREIGNTY — federation with single international personality vs. two-state "sovereign equality."
2. GOVERNANCE — power-sharing, presidency rotation, and central-government structure.
3. TERRITORY — Green Line adjustments, return of Varosha and Morphou, displaced-persons returns.
4. PROPERTY — dispossessed owners vs. current users; reinstatement or compensation.
5. SECURITY — 1960 guarantee/alliance treaties, foreign troop levels, UNFICYP mandate.
6. ENERGY — offshore gas rights in the Cypriot EEZ and freedom of navigation.
7. CITIZENSHIP — settlers, residency caps, and citizenship determination.
8. EU-STATUS — accession terms, acquis suspension in the north, and economic disparity.

## Procedure & rules

Sessions convene in plenary chaired by the UN Special Adviser of the Secretary-General on Cyprus (SASG). Opening and closing statements are public and on the record. Each delegation must protect its privileged instructions and may not disclose another party's confidential positions. Negotiations occur only in scheduled sessions; conduct in common areas is subject to intelligence collection.
