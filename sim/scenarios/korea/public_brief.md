# The Korean Peninsula: Resumption of the Six-Party Talks

## Current situation

The Korean War ended in a 1953 armistice, not a peace treaty, and the peninsula has remained formally at war ever since. The Six-Party Talks that ran from 2003 to 2008 produced the 2005 Joint Statement and then collapsed; the DPRK has since conducted repeated nuclear and ballistic-missile tests, placed a reconnaissance satellite in orbit, amended its constitution to define South Korea as a hostile state, demolished the inter-Korean road and rail links, and deepened a mutual-defense relationship with Russia that has included sending troops to the Kursk region. Sanctions — in particular recent U.S. measures targeting international financial institutions — have squeezed the North's economy, and Pyongyang insists they are producing a humanitarian crisis among its poorest citizens. Seoul, under a new government elected in June 2025, has halted loudspeaker broadcasts and called for restoring the Comprehensive Military Agreement; Tokyo's new prime minister has signalled willingness to meet Kim Jong-un over the abduction of Japanese citizens. In January 2026 President Xi Jinping announced the resumption of the Six-Party process, and on 1 April 2026 all six governments agreed the Chairman's Framework. Delegations from North Korea, South Korea, Japan, Russia and the United States arrived in Beijing on 2 May 2026; the talks open the following morning under a Chinese chair.

## Negotiation issues on the table (Chairman's Framework)

1. SECURITY-GUARANTEES — guarantees of the non-use of force by all sides; whether a Korean War peace treaty or inter-Korean reunification talks fall inside this process.
2. PEACEFUL-NUCLEAR-ENERGY — whether a DPRK returned to the NPT in good standing may hold a civilian nuclear program, and what must precede that discussion.
3. DIPLOMATIC-RELATIONS — normalization, and whether a formal end to the Korean War precedes or follows denuclearization.
4. SANCTIONS-HUMANITARIAN — sanctions relief, trade normalization, and the timing of humanitarian assistance.
5. DISARMAMENT — complete verified dismantlement in one comprehensive package versus a rewarded step-by-step sequence.
6. LWR-KEDO — light water reactors for power generation under a revived Korean Energy Development Organization, in exchange for closure of the North's nuclear program.

Issues raised at the table but outside the Framework: the "mutual freeze" and THAAD; DPRK proliferation, chemical and cyber capabilities; and the abduction of Japanese citizens.

## Procedure & rules

Sessions convene in plenary chaired by the Foreign Minister of the People's Republic of China, who chairs the opening and closing plenaries and any session called with all six parties. Opening and closing statements are on the record. Negotiations take place only in scheduled sessions; ad hoc bilateral and multilateral sessions are arranged through the control group and occur only if every invited party agrees to attend. Each delegation must protect its privileged instructions and may not disclose another party's confidential positions. Conduct in common or public areas is subject to intelligence collection.
