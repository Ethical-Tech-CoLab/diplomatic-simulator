# South China Sea Crisis Talks

## Current situation
An accelerating crisis over competing claims in the South China Sea (SCS) has brought the region close to armed confrontation. The waters carry roughly one-third of global maritime trade, hold about 10% of the annual global fisheries catch, and sit atop large untapped oil and gas reserves, making the disputed Spratly and Paracel islands, Scarborough Shoal, and Macclesfield Bank strategically vital. China asserts "indisputable sovereignty" over most of the sea based on its ten-dashed-line map and has conducted extensive land reclamation, building airfields, radar sites, and military outposts on contested features. Brunei, Malaysia, the Philippines, and Vietnam maintain overlapping claims, while Indonesia contests Chinese activity in its Natuna EEZ. Recent incidents, floating barriers at Scarborough Shoal, water-cannon and ramming of Philippine vessels, dueling reclamation projects, and expanded U.S. and allied Freedom of Navigation Operations, have sharply raised tensions. Extra-regional powers (the United States, India, and Japan) are drawn in by trade, alliance commitments, and freedom-of-navigation concerns, while ASEAN has struggled to reach a unified position. Convened by a UN Special Representative of the Secretary-General, delegations now gather in Royal City of Klang, Malaysia, to seek an equitable, non-military path forward.

## Negotiation issues on the table
1. SOVEREIGNTY — Competing territorial claims over the Spratly and Paracel islands, Scarborough Shoal, and related features.
2. UNCLOS — Whether reclaimed artificial "islands" qualify as islands granting 12nm territorial waters and 200nm EEZs.
3. NAVIGATION — Protecting freedom of navigation and innocent passage, including the status of FONOPS.
4. RESOURCES — Allocation and joint development of oil, gas, and seabed energy reserves.
5. FISHERIES — Access to and management of contested fishing grounds and coast-guard harassment.
6. MILITARIZATION — Halting or rolling back land reclamation, base-building, and military deployments.
7. ARBITRATION — Status of the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration award and its enforcement.
8. MULTILATERALISM — Bilateral versus ASEAN/UN multilateral frameworks and a binding code of conduct.

## Procedure & rules
The talks convene in plenary, chaired by the UN-/ASEAN-convened Special Representative of the Secretary-General presiding over the conference. Each delegation delivers a public opening statement, and all plenary statements are on the record. Substantive bargaining occurs only in scheduled bilateral and ad hoc sessions arranged through the control team; no negotiations take place outside formal sessions. Delegations must protect their privileged instructions, which are confidential communications from their governments, and remain in role throughout. Conversations in common or public areas are subject to intelligence collection.
