Today's policymakers are being asked to govern systems they cannot yet inspect. Models negotiate, agents transact, twins simulate, and ledgers settle value, increasingly without a human in the loop, and almost always faster than the regimes designed to oversee them. ETSAGP's leadership has named this gap directly: test-and-evaluation frameworks have not kept up with systems "designed to evolve in the wild, not in the lab," and the program's stated information-science scope, AI, autonomy, blockchain, encryption, is precisely where the public sector now most needs private-sector translators.

I have spent twenty years inside that gap as a builder. As Microsoft's Director of the Traceability Program and Cofounder of Microsoft Blockchain, I lead production-grade programs across the Microsoft Company-wide Traceability Program (multi-billion-dollar revenue exposure to all brands under UFLPA / NDAA, four global supply chains, spanning Compliance, Risk & Trade, and Tech for Fundamental Rights teams), the Supplychain Graph, the Digital Twin Systems Framework (DTFS), and the firm's exploration of agentic-commerce rails (x402). These are not prototypes; they are systems that move material, settle value, and bear regulatory weight today. As a Adjunct Professor of Global Affairs at NYU SPS Center for Global Affairs and founder of the Ethical Tech CoLab, I translate that work into the graduate course Harnessing Emerging Technology in Ethical Sourcing, anchored by the semesterly Ethical Tech Summit, where my students do hands-on research at the intersection of human conditions, informing policy with engineering.

Being an inaugural Nova Fellow is particularly meaningful to me: it is an opportunity to drive culture, set expectations for future cohorts, and demonstrate the value of bridging private-sector operational experience with public-sector policy design. The opportunity to shape the inaugural cohort's norms and expectations, while contributing to ETSAGP's Year-2 agenda, is rare and compelling. I want a Nova Fellowship for three reasons that map cleanly to your stated criteria.

Merit. Few candidates in the inaugural class can credibly speak to blockchain and verifiable compute as information-science primitives rather than as sectors or asset classes. I can, and I can demonstrate shipped systems behind every claim.

Impact. My fellowship deliverables (policy primer, Move37-aligned reference architecture & demo, and convening on critical supply chains) are designed to be load-bearing for ETSAGP's Year-2 expansion into biosecurity, futurecasting, supply chains, and mid-career policy-technology exchange, not adjacent to it. Each piece is co-publishable, co-convenable, and co-teachable from day one.

Commitment to global policy dialog. I have spent the last several years pulling private-sector practice into public conversations: UN CSW panels on technology and equity; UFLPA / NDAA traceability work alongside US federal stakeholders; industry partnerships on responsible deployment of agentic systems; and curriculum that places NYU graduate students directly into those conversations. A Nova Fellowship would sharpen that work through HKS-grade scrutiny, and contribute it back as durable artifacts to publish, convene around, and teach from.

Most importantly: I will be present. New York to Cambridge is straightforward, and I am committing to 30%+ on-campus presence.
